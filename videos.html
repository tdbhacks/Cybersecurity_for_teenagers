Fake News

Simple steps on how to spot fake news

Step 1: the basics

Check the author

If the story does not have one, you should be suspicious about it. Some fake news website do include the name of the author and a short biography, to convince you that the article is legitimate. However, most of the times you are not going to get any relevant results when googling the author's name, so this is one way to tell immediately if something sketchy is going on.

Check the date

Some false stories aren’t completely fake, but rather distortions of real events. These mendacious claims can take a legitimate news story and twist what it says — or even claim that something that happened long ago is related to current events.

Check the URL

The URL, which is the web address of the website visible on the top bar, can also be a way to tell if you are on a fake news website. In fact, several fake websites try disguise themselves as legitimate news websites like the New York Times, CNN, etc. Therefore, always check the URL in order to make sure you are actually visiting www.nytimes.com, instead of www.ny-times.com or some other slight variations.

Step 2: check your biases

Confirmation bias leads people to put more stock in information that confirms their beliefs and discount information that doesn’t. But the next time you’re automatically appalled at some Facebook post concerning, say, a politician you oppose, take a moment to check it out. Try this simple test: What other stories have been posted to the “news” website that is the source of the story that just popped up in your Facebook feed?

Step 3: Bookmark legitimate websites

The previous steps apply to "attracting" articles found on social networks and google, since they are the ones your unaware friends will share with you. But if you are actively browsing the web for news, there is one simple thing that will make your life easier: bookmark legitimate sources' websites, so that you can access them whenever you want to read what's going on in the world.
